Screenprinting Project

Introduction

In our New Media and Technology for Social Change class, we each came up with a design. The design had to involve a photo of ourselves or part of ourselves. The ultimate goal was to screenprint the design, and then push the paint through stencil onto our review card box and onto fabric, which would then be sewn together into a big canvas to display everyone's design.

Content Development and Photography

The first step towards screenprinting was to develop a concept, and then refine that concept. I decided to bring awareness to the mental impact of photojournalism in war zones, and to have the primary audience be myself and my classmates. I was inspired by Banksy to make something important to acknowledge. I used a picture of my fingers in a photo taking pose, as seen on the side of this paragraph, to represent photojournalism, and then edited a skull in the middle to represent the trauma experienced by photojournalists in warzones or disaster areas.

I used the text “REMEMBER THE TRAUMA” to remind people looking at the artwork of the meaning, and to emphasize the meaning. To get the photo of my fingers, a shallow depth of field was used to focus only on the hands, in an insert shot, which is where a part of the body is focused on. To achieve a shallow depth of field, and to retain good lighting, 1/500 shutter speed, which means the shutter closed in 1/500 of a second, and F4.5 aperture opening, which means that the apeture was large, were used. I also added in a skull from iStock. Here is the link to the skull:

Design Formalization

The next step toward screenprinting was to formalize the design in photoshop. I added the initial photo and the skull from istock as separate layers, and then separated the two hands into two layers, as well as getting rid of the background from the initial photo. The thumb in the top hand was skewed, so I rotated it to match the angle of the other thumb, but in doing so, had to fill in a gap. To do so, I used a generative AI fill to fill the gap. After that, I added the form before splitting it into two sections. I put “REMEMBER” in the bottom left of the image and “THE TRAUMA” in the top right of the image and I decided to color the text black. Once I had judged the image done, I had to make it ready to move on to the next step. To move on, I used the stamp filter to color half the image black, and the other half yellow. I then exported two PNGs, one yellow and one black, so the next step could be completed.

Making of the Frame, Stencil Preparation, and Screenprinting

After exporting the PNGs, the next step towards screenprinting was to determine the dimensions of the frames used to screenprint by using Illustrator. I made rectangles of successively larger size, until I was able to determine the lengths of the wood I needed to cut. I then used the saws to cut 8 pieces of wood to make my frames out of, as one frame was needed per color, and I was making a design with two colors.

Once I had the pieces cut, I glued them together with wood glue, and used staples to hold the frames together until the glue dried.

After the frames were dry, I stretched screens over the frames, held on with staples, to have a medium for the stencils to attach to. Next, I used Silhouette Studio to turn the single color PNGs into files able to be cut by the vinyl cutters, and cut my vinyl stencils. I had to “pick” my stencils, or remove the pieces blocking the paint from going into the wanted positive space, without removing areas not meant to be removed.

I then ironed the stencil onto my frames, and peeled off the plastic backing to get the stencil nearly ready to print. After that, I taped around my stencil to prevent paint leakage.

Then it was time to screenprint, so I put some paint on my black frame and pushed it through the frame with a squeegee onto paper, fabric, and the review card box. It took a few tries to get the result looking nice. Unfortunately, the yellow frame isn't working quite right, and I ran out of time to get the design looking how I wanted.
