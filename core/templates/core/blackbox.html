Black Box Investigation

Introduction

In our New Media and Technology for Social Change class we investigated the mystery black box. The mystery black box is a black trunk box with a hole cut in the top with a funnel in it, and a hole in the front with a piece of tubing sticking out. When pouring in the same amount of water, an inconsistent amount of water would come out. The goal of this investigation was to design and build a model to replicate the data we have from the mystery black box.

Claim

Inside the mystery black box, there are two separate tanks that are not directly connected.

Evidence

The mystery black box is a black trunk box with a funnel sticking into a hole in the top and a tube sticking out the front. When pouring in a consistent amount of water into the black box, an inconsistent amount of water came out.

For example, trial one poured in 1000 ml, and 830 ml came out, while trial two poured in 1000 ml, but 1300 ml came out. After more trials, it became clear that the mystery black box had more water in it than we had poured in, as the first four trials total output was 4105 ml, which is more than the total input of 4000 ml.

Table 1. Measurements From Original Black Box:

Trial Volume of Water Input (ml) Volume of Water output (ml) Time From All Water Poured in to When the Output Slows to a Drip (sec)
1 1000 830 39.6
2 1000 1300 1:31.02
3 1000 1400 1:05.32
4 1000 535 58.61
5 1000 1530 1:01.03
6 1000 200 51.55

My group decided that that must mean there is a second tank. We designed our model with two tanks connected by a valve, where the force of water in the first tank would open the valve, and allow some water from the second tank to exit the model.

We tested the model by filling up the second tank about halfway, and then pouring a small amount of water into the input. In trial one, we poured 395 ml in, and got 40 ml out. In trial two we poured in 675 ml, and 806 ml came out.

Reasoning

Models are representations of objects that are generally simpler and are used to show an idea or prove a theory. They can be useful, as they do not need to be polished or perfect, they only need to work. That also means they can be inaccurate or too simple as well. The evidence shows that more water comes out than is put in. Having a second tank could explain that, as that allows for more water to be stored than what is input. Our data does not disagree, as even though less water came out than what was put in, that was true for trials 4, 5, and 6 with the original black box. If there is one tank, the amount coming out would be more consistent. This is because the input and output would most likely be directly connected, rather than indirectly connected. A direct connection would mean that it would be harder for water to be stored, as water would want to continue downward. This is not what the data showed. This means our claim is more solid than one suggesting one tank.

3D Modeling, Animation, and Augmented Reality

I created my 3D model of my groups black box model in the 3D modeling software Autodesk Maya. I used the ideal version of our model, rather than how the physical model turned out as inspiration. To get a nice looking image, I used the proprietary renderer Arnold.

After creating the model, I animated the water moving through the model, causing the valve to open. It took a long time to get the values just right, and to get all the animations to line up

Personal Reflection

I came up with the idea that our black box model used as its basis, the idea of two tanks connected with a valve. I also came up with the design for the physical model and the design for the valve. Other contributions I made were the building of the valve, the making of the supply list, and photographer for multiple days.

I spent a long time in Autodesk Maya getting everything just right, including creating the idealized version of the black box model. I kept refining the design with smaller details, such as spheres representing water in the tube, and coordinated animations of water leveling changing the valve opening and closing, as you can see in the video above.

I learned that valve design is challenging, and valve building is even harder. I also learned how to use Autodesk Maya and that working in a team of people you do not know well is challenging, especially when you end up as the de facto leader. Some things that went well were the modeling phase of the digital black box model, making the collaboration contract, and using the sticky note progress tracking board.

I enjoyed using Autodesk Maya and making the website the most, as my prior knowledge and learning ability really shined in that part. I struggled with setlling for an animation that wasn't really what I wanted and with building a succesful valve. I could think about issues that may arise as I design, rather than as I build, and I could better learn how to manage helping others, while also getting my work done.

21st Century Competency

The 21st century competency I demonstrated the most was creativity and innovation. Specifically, I demonstrated orginality and inventiveness throughout the project. I created a valve design that could, in theory, work using cardboard and waterproof tape. I also invented a way to represent water spreading out in a container without too much work or liquid simulation.
